Large language models are increasingly used to support scientific discovery, but their
apparent fluency can obscure important limitations. In particular, benchmark performance
does not guarantee that a model will remain reliable when the distribution of inputs changes.
Researchers should therefore report uncertainty, examine failure cases, and distinguish
between evidence produced by an experiment and conclusions inferred from that evidence.

For example, an improvement from 82.4 percent to 83.1 percent may be statistically detectable
without being practically meaningful. The interpretation depends on sample size, measurement
error, the cost of mistakes, and whether the evaluation data represent the intended population.
